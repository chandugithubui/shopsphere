# Product Requirement Document (PRD) - Interview Questions

---

## Q1. What is a Product Requirement Document (PRD)?

Answer:

A Product Requirement Document (PRD) is a document that defines the purpose, business goals, features, user requirements, and expected behavior of a software product before development begins. It acts as a bridge between business stakeholders and the development team.

---

## Q2. Why is a PRD important?

Answer:

A PRD ensures that all stakeholders have a common understanding of the product, reduces misunderstandings, minimizes rework, and provides a clear roadmap for development.

---

## Q3. What is the difference between a PRD and an SRS?

Answer:

A PRD focuses on business needs and product features (what to build), while an SRS focuses on technical implementation details (how to build it).

---

## Q4. What are Functional Requirements?

Answer:

Functional Requirements define the features and functionalities that the system must provide, such as user registration, login, product search, shopping cart, and order management.

---

## Q5. What are Non-Functional Requirements?

Answer:

Non-Functional Requirements define the quality attributes of the system, such as performance, security, scalability, availability, maintainability, and responsiveness.

---

## Q6. Explain the difference between Functional and Non-Functional Requirements.

Answer:

Functional Requirements describe **what** the system should do, whereas Non-Functional Requirements describe **how well** the system should perform.

---

## Q7. What is a Stakeholder?

Answer:

A stakeholder is any individual, team, or organization that has an interest in the project or is affected by its outcome. Examples include customers, administrators, developers, QA engineers, and business owners.

---

## Q8. Who are the primary users of ShopSphere?

Answer:

ShopSphere has two primary user roles:

- Customer
- Administrator

---

## Q9. What is Project Scope?

Answer:

Project Scope defines the features and functionality that are included in the current release and clearly identifies what is outside the scope.

---

## Q10. What is an MVP?

Answer:

A Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is the first functional version of a product that includes only the essential features needed to solve the core business problem.

---

## Q11. Why do companies build an MVP first?

Answer:

Building an MVP allows companies to deliver value quickly, gather user feedback, validate the product idea, reduce development risk, and prioritize future enhancements based on real user needs.

---

## Q12. What are Project Assumptions?

Answer:

Project Assumptions are conditions considered to be true during planning and development, allowing the team to proceed without waiting for every detail to be finalized.

---

## Q13. What are Project Constraints?

Answer:

Project Constraints are limitations such as time, budget, technology, resources, and scope that influence project planning and execution.

---

## Q14. What are Success Criteria?

Answer:

Success Criteria are measurable outcomes used to determine whether the project has achieved its business and technical objectives.

---

## Q15. What are Acceptance Criteria?

Answer:

Acceptance Criteria define the conditions that a feature must satisfy before it is considered complete and accepted by stakeholders.

---

## Q16. What is the difference between Success Criteria and Acceptance Criteria?

Answer:

Success Criteria evaluate the success of the overall project, while Acceptance Criteria evaluate whether an individual feature is complete and meets its expected behavior.

---

## Q17. Why did you prepare a PRD before coding?

Answer:

Preparing a PRD helped define the project goals, identify stakeholders and users, document functional and non-functional requirements, establish project scope, and create a clear roadmap for development. This reduces ambiguity and improves communication throughout the project lifecycle.

---

## Q18. How did the PRD help in planning ShopSphere?

Answer:

The PRD provided a structured understanding of the business objectives, target users, system features, quality requirements, constraints, and future roadmap. It serves as the foundation for the SRS, architecture, database design, API planning, and implementation.

---

## Interview Tip

When discussing ShopSphere in an interview, don't just list features. Explain that you followed a structured process:

1. Requirement Analysis
2. Product Requirement Document (PRD)
3. Software Requirement Specification (SRS)
4. Software Architecture
5. Database Design
6. API Design
7. Development
8. Testing
9. Deployment

This demonstrates that you understand the complete Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC), not just coding.
